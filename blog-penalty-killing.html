Penalty Killing: Defensive Systeme im Vergleich (Box, Diamond, Wedge+1)

Eine gute PK-Quote rettet Spiele. Teams, die im Unterzahl-Spiel über 82–83 % halten, haben über eine Saison hinweg deutliche Tabellenvorteile. Dabei ist das System allein entscheidend – Kommunikation, Reaktionsgeschwindigkeit und Stick-Placement sind ebenso wichtig. Dieser Artikel vergleicht die drei gängigsten PK-Systeme, erklärt ihre Vor- und Nachteile und zeigt, welche Situationen welches System begünstigen.

Das Box-System: Solide Basis für jeden

Die Box-Formation ist das am weitesten verbreitete PK-System, weil sie einfach zu verstehen und robust gegen die meisten Powerplay-Varianten ist. Vier Spieler bilden ein Rechteck: Zwei vordere Spieler auf der Höhe der oberen Bullykreise, zwei hintere Spieler zwischen Bullykreisen und Torraum. Die Box schiebt kollektiv in Richtung des Pucks, bleibt aber immer als Einheit zusammen.

Grundprinzipien der Box

  • Die Box dreht sich immer in Richtung Puck – der ballnahe Spieler schiebt auf, der ballfernseitige rückt ein.
  • Kein Spieler verlässt die Box-Formation, bis der Puck eindeutig auf die andere Seite gespielt wurde.
  • Die vorderen Spieler verengen aktiv den Point-Spieler – kein freier Schuss von der blauen Linie.
  • Die hinteren Spieler blocken Pässe in den Slot und decken die Flanken gegen Querpässe.

Stärken: Diszipliniert, schwer zu durchbrechen, funktioniert auch mit weniger athletischen Spielern. Schwächen: Passives System – das PP kontrolliert den Rhythmus. Bumper-Spieler auf der Halbposition haben oft freien Schuss, wenn die Box schlecht komprimiert.

Das Diamond-System: Aggressiver und variabler

Beim Diamond steht ein Spieler vorne an der blauen Linie (Point-Pressure), ein Spieler links, einer rechts (auf Halbhöhe), und einer hinten direkt vor dem Torwart. Dieses System ist offensiver: Der vordere Spieler kann den PP-Point unter Druck setzen und Pässe abfangen.

Vor- und Nachteile des Diamond

VorteilNachteil
Aktiver Druck auf den PointAnfälliger für Querpässe in die Flanken
Abfangen von Pässen möglichErfordert schnelle, athletische Spieler
Stört PP-Aufbau frühKann bei schlechter Kommunikation auseinanderfallen
Gut gegen 1-3-1-PPSchwach gegen Overload auf einer Seite

Der Diamond eignet sich besonders gegen Teams mit einem dominanten Point-Spieler (Schuss von der blauen Linie als primäre Bedrohung). Durch aktiven Druck wird dieser Spieler aus seinem Rhythmus gebracht.

Wedge+1: Für aggressive PK-Teams

Der Wedge+1 kombiniert eine passive Dreiecksformation (Wedge) hinten mit einem aggressiven Vorderläufer, der früh und persistent auf den Puckträger forecheckt. Ziel ist es, das PP unter Zeitdruck zu setzen und Turnover zu erzwingen. Wenn der Vorderläufer den Puck gewinnt, entsteht ein 2-on-1 oder sogar ein Breakaway.

Diese Variante ist risikoreicher: Gelingt der Forecheck nicht, ist das PK-Team eine Position kurz. Der Wedge+1 funktioniert gut bei Teams mit einer starken PK-Einheit aus athletischen, konditionell starken Spielern. Im Amateureishockey ab Regionalliga-Niveau einsetzbar; für Jugendteams meist zu komplex.

Stick-Lane-Kontrolle: Die unterschätzte Mikrotechnik

Unabhängig vom System ist Stick-Placement das wichtigste Detail. Ein gut positionierter Stick im Passweg ist wertvoller als ein Body-Check. Die Regeln für Stick-Lane-Kontrolle:

  • Stick liegt immer auf dem Eis – nie hochgehalten, solange der Puck nicht in Schussposition ist.
  • Blade zeigt in die wahrscheinlichste Passroute, nicht zum Puckträger.
  • Beim Vorschieben auf den Puckträger: Stick in die Schusslinie bringen, nicht wild schwingen.
  • Slot-Pässe abdecken hat Priorität vor Schussblocking von außen.

Trainingsstudie aus dem Juniorenbereich: Teams, die drei Trainingseinheiten ausschließlich Stick-Placement gewidmet hatten, reduzierten ihre PP-Goals-against-Rate um 18 % über die folgende Saisonphase.

Clearing-Optionen: Puck raus, Druck weg

Puck-Clearing ist die häufigste und wichtigste PK-Aktion außer Schussblocking. Drei Varianten:

  1. Direktes Icing: Möglich und legal im Unterzahl (außer in manchen Jugendligen). Puck hart von der eigenen Hälfte über die rote Linie schießen. Simpler als es klingt – trainiere explizit Clearing unter Drucksituationen.
  2. Klarer Pass auf den eigenen Spieler: Wenn möglich, Puck auf einen freien Mitspieler spielen, der die Zone verlässt (Zweck: Faceoff erzwingen oder Zeit schinden). Risiko: Fehlpass im eigenen Drittel.
  3. Dump zur Bande und Forecheck: Puck in die Ecke schlagen und sofort zwei Spieler hinterher. Selten im PK, aber bei geschwächtem PP-Aufbau manchmal die beste Option.

Forecheck-Pressure im PK: Wann lohnt es sich?

Aggressive PK-Teams wie die historisch starken PK-Einheiten der Boston Bruins oder Detroit Red Wings haben gezeigt: Ein gesunder Forecheck-Anteil im PK (30–40 % der Situationen) verbessert die PK-Quote messbar, weil er Turnover erzwingt. Die Bedingung: Nur forecheckst du aktiv, wenn der PP-Puckträger unter echtem Zeitdruck ist – also an der Bande gefangen, mit dem Rücken zur Spielfläche, oder nach einer ungenauen Querpassaufnahme.

Forecheckst du immer und überall, verlierst du die Formation. Die beste PK-Forechecker kommunizieren klar: „Ich geh!" – und alle anderen rücken sofort in die Box-Lücke nach.

Icing-Taktik und Shot-Block-Entscheidungen

Icing ist im PK eine Waffe, keine Niederlage. Trainiere Spieler, Icing als taktisches Mittel zu nutzen und nicht zu vermeiden. Faustregel: Ist der Weg zum Tor zu weit und keine freie Option erkennbar, lieber Icing als riskanter Pass.

Shot-Blocking: Konsequentes Shot-Blocking reduziert PP-Schüsse aufs Tor um 20–25 %. Aber: Blindes Hinstellen ist gefährlich (Verletzungsrisiko). Trainingsregel – Block only if path to puck is clear and you're between puck and goal. Kein Blocking bei Querpässen in Bewegung; erst wenn der Schuss abgesetzt wird.

Penalty Killing: Defensive Systems Compared (Box, Diamond, Wedge+1)

A strong PK percentage saves games. Teams that hold their penalty killing above 82–83% have measurable standings advantages over a full season. The system alone isn't everything — communication, reaction speed, and stick placement matter just as much. This article compares the three most common PK systems, explains their pros and cons, and shows which situations favor which approach.

The Box System: A Solid Foundation for Everyone

The box formation is the most widely used PK system because it's easy to understand and robust against most power play variations. Four players form a rectangle: two forwards at the top of the faceoff circles, two defenders between the circles and the crease. The box shifts collectively toward the puck but always stays together as a unit.

Core Principles of the Box

  • The box always rotates toward the puck — the near-side player pushes out, the far-side player slides in.
  • No player leaves the box formation until the puck has clearly moved to the other side.
  • The front players actively close down the point man — no free shot from the blue line.
  • The back players block passes into the slot and cover the flanks against cross-ice feeds.

Strengths: disciplined, hard to break down, works even with less athletic players. Weaknesses: passive system — the power play controls the rhythm. A bumper player in the half-wall often has a free shot if the box compresses poorly.

The Diamond System: More Aggressive and Variable

In the diamond, one player stands at the blue line (applying point pressure), one is left, one right (at mid-height), and one directly in front of the goaltender. This system is more offensive: the front player can pressure the PP point and intercept passes.

Pros and Cons of the Diamond

AdvantageDisadvantage
Active pressure on the pointMore vulnerable to cross-ice feeds
Can intercept passesRequires quick, athletic players
Disrupts PP setup earlyCan fall apart with poor communication
Effective against 1-3-1 PPWeak against overloads on one side

The diamond is particularly well-suited against teams with a dominant point player (blue-line shot as the primary threat). Active pressure disrupts that player's rhythm.

Wedge+1: For Aggressive PK Units

The Wedge+1 combines a passive rear triangle (wedge) with an aggressive front chaser who forechecks early and persistently on the puck carrier. The goal is to put the power play under time pressure and force turnovers. When the forechecker wins the puck, it can create a 2-on-1 or even a breakaway.

This variation is higher-risk: if the forecheck fails, the PK unit is temporarily short one player. The Wedge+1 works well for teams with a strong PK unit of athletic, conditioned skaters. Applicable at the regional-league level and above; typically too complex for youth teams.

Stick-Lane Control: The Underrated Micro-Skill

Regardless of system, stick placement is the most important detail. A well-positioned stick in the passing lane is more valuable than a body check. Rules for stick-lane control:

  • Stick always on the ice — never lifted while the puck isn't in shooting position.
  • Blade points toward the most likely passing route, not toward the puck carrier.
  • When closing on the puck carrier: get the stick into the shooting lane, don't swipe wildly.
  • Covering slot passes takes priority over blocking outside shots.

A training study from the junior level: teams that devoted three sessions exclusively to stick placement reduced their PP goals-against rate by 18% over the following portion of the season.

Clearing Options: Get the Puck Out, Relieve the Pressure

Puck clearing is the most frequent and important PK action outside of shot-blocking. Three options:

  1. Direct icing: Legal and permitted when shorthanded (except in some youth leagues). Shoot the puck hard from your own half past the red line. Simpler than it sounds — practice clearing explicitly under pressure situations.
  2. Clean pass to a teammate: When possible, play the puck to a free teammate exiting the zone (purpose: force an icing or buy time). Risk: errant pass in your own end.
  3. Dump to the boards and forecheck: Chip the puck into the corner and immediately send two players after it. Rare on the PK, but sometimes the best option against a weakened PP setup.

Forecheck Pressure on the PK: When Is It Worth It?

Aggressive PK units — like the historically strong units of the Boston Bruins or Detroit Red Wings — have shown that a healthy forecheck component on the PK (30–40% of situations) measurably improves the PK percentage by forcing turnovers. The condition: only forecheck aggressively when the PP puck carrier is under genuine time pressure — pinned to the boards, back to the ice surface, or after a mishandled cross-pass reception.

Forechecking everywhere and always breaks the formation. The best PK forecheckers communicate clearly: "I'm going!" — and everyone else immediately closes the gap in the box.

Icing Tactics and Shot-Block Decisions

Icing is a weapon on the PK, not a defeat. Train players to use icing as a tactical tool, not to avoid it. Rule of thumb: if the path to the net is too long and no clear option is available, icing is better than a risky pass.

Shot-blocking: consistent shot-blocking reduces PP shots on goal by 20–25%. But: blindly throwing yourself in front of shots is dangerous (injury risk). Training rule — block only if the path to the puck is clear and you're between the puck and the goal. No blocking during moving cross-passes; only once the shot is being taken.

Which System for Which Situation?

SituationRecommended System
Opponent with strong point player (heavy shooting)Diamond or Box with active front player
Opponent with 1-3-1 and bumper dominanceBox with strict bumper coverage
Opponent with overload PPBox, shift toward the overload side
Tired team, needs securityBox — no experiments
Athletic team, needs turnoversWedge+1 as a surprise system

The box shifts collectively toward the puck but always stays together as a unit.

Core Principles of the Box

  • The box always rotates toward the puck — the near-side player pushes out, the far-side player slides in.
  • No player leaves the box formation until the puck has clearly moved to the other side.
  • The front players actively close down the point man — no free shot from the blue line.
  • The back players block passes into the slot and cover the flanks against cross-ice feeds.

Strengths: disciplined, hard to break down, works even with less athletic players. Weaknesses: passive system — the большинство controls the rhythm. A bumper player in the half-wall often has a free shot if the box compresses poorly.

The Diamond System: More Aggressive and Variable

In the diamond, one player stands at the blue line (applying point pressure), one is left, one right (at mid-height), and one directly in front of the вратарь. This system is more offensive: the front player can pressure the PP point and intercept passes.

The diamond is particularly well-suited against teams with a dominant point player (blue-line shot as the primary threat). Active pressure disrupts that player's rhythm.

Wedge+1: For Aggressive PK Units

The Wedge+1 combines a passive rear triangle (wedge) with an aggressive front chaser who forechecks early and persistently on the puck carrier. The goal is to put the большинство under time pressure and force turnovers. When the forechecker wins the puck, it can create a 2-on-1 or even a breakaway.

This variation is higher-risk: if the forecheck fails, the PK unit is temporarily short one player. The Wedge+1 works well for teams with a strong PK unit of athletic, conditioned skaters. Applicable at the regional-league level and above; typically too complex for youth teams.

Stick-Lane Control: The Underrated Micro-Skill

Regardless of system, stick placement is the most important detail. A well-positioned stick in the passing lane is more valuable than a body check. Rules for stick-lane control:

  • Stick always on the ice — never lifted while the puck isn't in shooting position.
  • Blade points toward the most likely passing route, not toward the puck carrier.
  • When closing on the puck carrier: get the stick into the shooting lane, don't swipe wildly.
  • Covering slot passes takes priority over blocking outside shots.

A training study from the junior level: teams that devoted three sessions exclusively to stick placement reduced their PP goals-against rate by 18% over the following portion of the season.

Clearing Options: Get the Puck Out, Relieve the Pressure

Puck clearing is the most frequent and important PK action outside of shot-blocking. Three options:

  1. Direct icing: Legal and permitted when shorthanded (except in some youth leagues). Shoot the puck hard from your own half past the red line. Simpler than it sounds — practice clearing explicitly under pressure situations.
  2. Clean pass to a teammate: When possible, play the puck to a free teammate exiting the zone (purpose: force an icing or buy time). Risk: errant pass in your own end.
  3. Dump to the boards and forecheck: Chip the puck into the corner and immediately send two players after it. Rare on the PK, but sometimes the best option against a weakened PP setup.

Forecheck Pressure on the PK: When Is It Worth It?

Aggressive PK units — like the historically strong units of the Boston Bruins or Detroit Red Wings — have shown that a healthy forecheck component on the PK (30–40% of situations) measurably improves the PK percentage by forcing turnovers. The condition: only forecheck aggressively when the PP puck carrier is under genuine time pressure — pinned to the boards, back to the ice surface, or after a mishandled cross-pass reception.

Forechecking everywhere and always breaks the formation. The best PK forecheckers communicate clearly: "I'm going!" — and everyone else immediately closes the gap in the box.

Icing Tactics and Shot-Block Decisions

Icing is a weapon on the PK, not a defeat. Train players to use icing as a tactical tool, not to avoid it. Rule of thumb: if the path to the net is too long and no clear option is available, icing is better than a risky pass.

Shot-blocking: consistent shot-blocking reduces PP shots on goal by 20–25%. But: blindly throwing yourself in front of shots is dangerous (injury risk). Training rule — block only if the path to the puck is clear and you're between the puck and the goal. No blocking during moving cross-passes; only once the shot is being taken.
