Torwart-Analyse: 12 Kennzahlen jenseits der Fangquote

Die Fangquote (Save Percentage, SV%) ist die bekannteste Torwart-Statistik – und gleichzeitig eine der irreführendsten. Ein Torwart mit SV% .915, der nur Schüsse von außen abwehrt, leistet deutlich weniger als einer mit SV% .905, der ständig in High-Danger-Situationen gefordert wird. Wer Torhüter wirklich bewerten will, braucht mehr Perspektiven. Dieser Artikel stellt 12 Kennzahlen vor, erklärt ihren Nutzen und zeigt, welche auch ohne Profi-Software erfasst werden können.

1. Save Percentage (SV%) – Basis und Grenzen

SV% = Gehaltene Schüsse / Schüsse gesamt. Liga-Durchschnitt in der DEL: etwa .910–.915. Der Wert ist leicht zu ermitteln, aber teamabhängig: Ein schwaches Defensivteam mit vielen Slot-Schüssen gegen macht den Torwart schlechter aussehen, als er ist. SV% ist deshalb immer im Kontext der Schussqualität gegen zu betrachten.

2. Goals Saved Above Expected (GSAx)

GSAx = Erwartete Gegentore (basierend auf xG) minus tatsächliche Gegentore. Ein GSAx von +5,0 bedeutet, der Torwart hat fünf Tore mehr gehalten, als statistisch erwartet wurden. Ein GSAx von -3,0 bedeutet, er hat drei Tore mehr kassiert als erwartet. Dies ist die präziseste Kennzahl für individuelle Torwartleistung, weil sie den Einfluss der eigenen Defensivstruktur herausrechnet.

Im Amateurbereich lässt sich GSAx vereinfacht schätzen: Schüsse aus dem Slot gegen zählen als „erwartete Tore" mit 0,17, Schüsse von der blauen Linie mit 0,04. Die Summe ergibt die erwarteten Gegentore; Differenz zur Realität = vereinfachter GSAx.

3. High-Danger Save Percentage (HDSV%)

HDSV% misst, wie viele Schüsse aus der Hochgefahrenzone (Slot und untere Bullykreise) der Torwart hält. Durchschnittlich werden etwa 15–20 % dieser Schüsse zu Toren. Ein Torwart mit HDSV% .820 liegt im Mittelfeld; .850+ ist Spitzenklasse. Diese Zahl sagt weit mehr aus als das Gesamtergebnis, weil sie gezielt das kritischste Segment bewertet.

4. Low-Danger Save Percentage (LDSV%)

Schüsse von außen, von der blauen Linie, aus spitzen Winkeln. Ein Ligadurchschnitt von .975–.985 ist normal; wer hier auffällig niedrig liegt, hat möglicherweise Konzentrationsprobleme in vermeintlich einfachen Situationen. Für Trainer: Analysiere, ob Gegentore aus Low-Danger-Zonen auf Unaufmerksamkeit oder fehlerhaftes Positioning hindeuten.

5. Rebound Control Rate

Wie oft hält ein Torwart den Puck sicher, statt ihn unkontrolliert abprallen zu lassen? Professionelle Torwarte kontrollieren ca. 60–70 % ihrer Schüsse sicher (kein Rebound). Rebounds führen direkt zu den gefährlichsten Folgesituationen. Schlechte Rebound-Kontrolle ist oft ein Technik-Problem: Zu flaches Pad-Winkel, falsche Handposition oder fehlendes „Puck-Absorbing" mit dem Fanghandschuh.

6. Lateral Movement Assessment

Die Fähigkeit, quer zu scramblen – also bei Querpässen im Slot schnell die Position zu wechseln. Wird in Trainingseinheiten durch den „Post-to-Post"-Drill gemessen: Zeit von einem Pfosten zum anderen in vollständiger Schutzposition. Ein gutes Ergebnis für Amateure: unter 1,2 Sekunden bei korrekter Technik. Defizite hier sind oft auf schwache Hüftflexoren und fehlendes T-Push-Training zurückzuführen.

7. Tiefe im Crease (Depth Management)

Zu tief im Tor stehen bedeutet größere Winkel für den Schützen. Zu weit vorne bedeutet schlechte Reaktionszeit und Anfälligkeit für Chips über die Schulter. Die optimale Tiefe variiert je nach Schusswinkel. Trainer können dies einfach messen: Schnurmarkierungen im Training, Videoanalyse aus der Seitenperspektive. Viele Junioren-Torwarte stehen 15–20 cm zu tief, was die Winkelabdeckung signifikant verschlechtert.

8. Screen-Situationen: SV% vor Screens

Schüsse mit Spieler davor sind für jeden Torwart schwerer. Der Unterschied zwischen Spitzentorhütern und Durchschnitt liegt oft hier: Gute Torhüter kommunizieren früh mit Verteidigern, suchen aktiv den Puck um den Screen herum und verlagern ihre Ausgangsposition bei erkennbaren Screen-Läufen. Trainer sollten Screen-Situationen explizit trainieren – mindestens einmal pro Woche in der Vorbereitung.

9. Five-Hole-Tendenz

Der Five-Hole – der Bereich zwischen den Beinschienen – ist bei vielen Torhütern die Schwachstelle, besonders nach schnellen Lateral-Bewegungen, wenn die Pads sich nicht komplett schließen. Schüsse, die den Torwart in Bewegung bringen und dann auf den Five-Hole abgeschlossen werden, sind für viele Amateur-Torhüter kaum zu halten. Analyse: Welcher Anteil der kassierten Tore geht durch den Five-Hole? Liegt er über 25 %, ist gezieltes Pad-Closing-Training angesagt.

10. Blocker/Glove-Asymmetrie

Fast alle Torhüter haben eine schwächere Seite. Eine Asymmetrie von mehr als 8 Prozentpunkten zwischen Fanghand-SV% und Blocker-SV% ist ein klares Trainingssignal. Gegner auf höherem Niveau erkennen solche Tendenzen schnell. Konkrete Übung: Schussserien ausschließlich auf die schwächere Seite, mit zunehmender Schussgeschwindigkeit und -variation.

11. Cold Start vs. Hot Goalie

Wie unterscheidet sich die Leistung in den ersten fünf Minuten eines Spiels von der Leistung nach 30 Minuten? Manche Torhüter brauchen 8–10 Minuten, um auf Betriebstemperatur zu kommen – statistisch erkennbar an erhöhter Gegentorquote in den ersten Minuten. Das lässt sich durch eine spezifische Warm-Up-Routine adressieren: mehr intensive Schussserien, mehr Lateral-Bewegungen, explizite Mental-Aktivierung vor dem Anpfiff.

12. Penalty-Shot-Performance

Penalty Shots enden in der NHL in etwa 32 % der Fälle mit einem Tor – im Amateur-Eishockey variiert diese Quote stark (20–45 %). Ein Torwart, der drei von fünf Penalty Shots hält, hat damit oft mehr zum Saisonefolg beigetragen als durch zehn weitere Fangquoten-Prozentpunkte im Spielbetrieb. Training für Penalty Shots: Analyse von Schützen-Tendenz (Forehand/Backhand, Tempo-Variationen), eigene Reaktions-Drills, Kommunikation mit dem Schützen durch Lesen der Hüften statt des Pucks.

Tracking-Methoden für Amateurtrainer

Nicht alle 12 Kennzahlen lassen sich gleichzeitig erfassen. Empfohlene Priorität für Amateurteams:

  1. SV% und HDSV% (realisierbar mit Shot-Map + Ergebnisprotokoll)
  2. Rebound Control Rate (visuell schätzbar, Videoanalyse hilfreich)
  3. Tiefe im Crease (einmal pro Trainingsblock mit Markierungen prüfen)
  4. Blocker/Glove-Asymmetrie (nach 5–6 Spielen auswertbar)
  5. Cold-Start-Tendenz (erste 5 vs. Rest der Spielzeit auswerten)

Selbst mit einem einfachen Notizbuch und einem aufmerksamen Assistenten lassen sich diese Daten zuverlässig erfassen. Die daraus entstehenden Erkenntnisse übersteigen den Nutzen jeder subjektiven Beurteilung nach dem Spiel.

Goaltender Analysis: 12 Metrics Beyond Save Percentage

Save percentage (SV%) is the best-known goaltender statistic — and simultaneously one of the most misleading. A goalie with a .915 SV% who only faces outside shots delivers far less than one with a .905 SV% who is constantly tested in high-danger situations. Anyone who wants to truly evaluate a goaltender needs more perspectives. This article introduces 12 metrics, explains their value, and shows which can be tracked even without professional software.

1. Save Percentage (SV%) — Foundation and Limitations

SV% = saves / total shots. League average in the DEL: approximately .910–.915. The number is easy to calculate but team-dependent: a weak defensive unit generating many slot shots against makes a goalie look worse than he is. SV% must always be viewed in the context of shot quality against.

2. Goals Saved Above Expected (GSAx)

GSAx = expected goals allowed (based on xG) minus actual goals allowed. A GSAx of +5.0 means the goaltender saved five more goals than statistically expected. A GSAx of -3.0 means he allowed three more goals than expected. This is the most precise metric for individual goaltender performance because it removes the influence of the team's defensive structure.

At the amateur level, GSAx can be estimated in simplified form: shots from the slot against count as "expected goals" at 0.17, shots from the blue line at 0.04. The sum gives the expected goals allowed; the difference from reality = simplified GSAx.

3. High-Danger Save Percentage (HDSV%)

HDSV% measures how many shots from the high-danger zone (slot and lower faceoff circles) the goaltender stops. On average, about 15–20% of these shots result in goals. A goalie with HDSV% .820 is in the middle of the pack; .850+ is elite. This number tells far more than the overall result because it specifically evaluates the most critical segment.

4. Low-Danger Save Percentage (LDSV%)

Shots from outside, from the blue line, from tight angles. A league average of .975–.985 is normal; anyone notably below this may have concentration issues in seemingly routine situations. For coaches: analyze whether goals from low-danger zones point to inattention or poor positioning.

5. Rebound Control Rate

How often does a goaltender cleanly secure the puck, rather than letting it rebound uncontrolled? Professional goalies control approximately 60–70% of their shots cleanly (no rebound). Rebounds lead directly to the most dangerous follow-up situations. Poor rebound control is often a technical problem: too-shallow pad angle, incorrect hand position, or insufficient "puck absorption" with the glove.

6. Lateral Movement Assessment

The ability to scramble across — quickly changing position on cross-slot passes. Measured in practice through the "post-to-post" drill: time from one post to the other in full save position. A good result for amateurs: under 1.2 seconds with correct technique. Deficiencies here often stem from weak hip flexors and insufficient T-push training.

7. Depth Management (Crease Depth)

Standing too deep in the crease gives the shooter wider angles. Standing too far out means poor reaction time and vulnerability to chips over the shoulder. Optimal depth varies by shot angle. Coaches can measure this easily: cord markings in practice, video analysis from the side angle. Many junior goalies stand 15–20 cm too deep, which significantly worsens angle coverage.

8. Screen Situations: SV% in Front of Screens

Shots with a player in front are harder for every goaltender. The difference between elite goalies and average ones often lies here: good goaltenders communicate early with their defensemen, actively look for the puck around the screen, and adjust their starting position when screen runs are recognizable. Coaches should explicitly practice screen situations — at least once per week during preparation.

9. Five-Hole Tendency

The five-hole — the area between the pads — is a weak spot for many goaltenders, particularly after quick lateral movements when the pads don't close completely. Shots that draw the goalie into motion and then finish on the five-hole are nearly impossible for many amateur goalies to stop. Analysis: what percentage of goals allowed go through the five-hole? If it's above 25%, targeted pad-closing training is needed.

10. Blocker/Glove Asymmetry

Almost all goaltenders have a weaker side. An asymmetry of more than 8 percentage points between glove-side SV% and blocker-side SV% is a clear training signal. Opponents at higher levels quickly identify such tendencies. Concrete drill: shot series exclusively to the weaker side, with increasing shot speed and variation.

11. Cold Start vs. Hot Goalie

How does performance in the first five minutes of a game compare to the 30-minute mark? Some goaltenders need 8–10 minutes to reach operating temperature — statistically evident in a higher goals-allowed rate in the early minutes. This can be addressed through a specific warm-up routine: more intensive shot series, more lateral movements, explicit mental activation before puck drop.

12. Penalty Shot Performance

Penalty shots result in goals approximately 32% of the time in the NHL — in amateur hockey, this rate varies considerably (20–45%). A goalie who stops three of five penalty shots has often contributed more to season success than ten extra SV% points in regular play. Training for penalty shots: analyze shooter tendencies (forehand/backhand, speed variations), individual reaction drills, reading hips rather than watching the puck.

Tracking Methods for Amateur Coaches

Not all 12 metrics can be captured simultaneously. Recommended priority for amateur teams:

  1. SV% and HDSV% (achievable with a shot map + game log)
  2. Rebound control rate (can be estimated visually, video analysis helpful)
  3. Crease depth (check once per training block with markers)
  4. Blocker/glove asymmetry (assessable after 5–6 games)
  5. Cold-start tendency (compare first 5 minutes vs. rest of game)

Even with a simple notebook and an attentive assistant, this data can be reliably captured. The insights it produces exceed the value of any subjective post-game assessment.

A maalivahti who stops three of five penalty shots has often contributed more to season success than ten extra SV% points in regular play. Training for penalty shots: analyze shooter tendencies (forehand/backhand, speed variations), individual reaction drills, reading hips rather than watching the puck.

Tracking Methods for Amateur Coaches

Not all 12 metrics can be captured simultaneously. Recommended priority for amateur teams:

  1. SV% and HDSV% (achievable with a shot map + game log)
  2. Rebound control rate (can be estimated visually, video analysis helpful)
  3. Crease depth (check once per training block with markers)
  4. Blocker/glove asymmetry (assessable after 5–6 games)
  5. Cold-start tendency (compare first 5 minutes vs. rest of game)

Even with a simple notebook and an attentive assistant, this data can be reliably captured. The insights it produces exceed the value of any subjective post-game assessment.

Goaltender Analysis: 12 Metrics Beyond Save Percentage

Save percentage (SV%) is the best-known вратарь statistic — and simultaneously one of the most misleading. A вратарь with a .915 SV% who only faces outside shots delivers far less than one with a .905 SV% who is constantly tested in high-danger situations. Anyone who wants to truly evaluate a вратарь needs more perspectives. This article introduces 12 metrics, explains their value, and shows which can be tracked even without professional software.

1. Save Percentage (SV%) — Foundation and Limitations

SV% = saves / total shots. League average in the DEL: approximately .910–.915. The number is easy to calculate but team-dependent: a weak defensive unit generating many slot shots against makes a вратарь look worse than he is. SV% must always be viewed in the context of shot quality against.

2. Goals Saved Above Expected (GSAx)

GSAx = expected goals allowed (based on xG) minus actual goals allowed. A GSAx of +5.0 means the вратарь saved five more goals than statistically expected. A GSAx of -3.0 means he allowed three more goals than expected. This is the most precise metric for individual вратарь performance because it removes the influence of the team's defensive structure.

At the amateur level, GSAx can be estimated in simplified form: shots from the slot against count as "expected goals" at 0.17, shots from the blue line at 0.04. The sum gives the expected goals allowed; the difference from reality = simplified GSAx.

3. High-Danger Save Percentage (HDSV%)

HDSV% measures how many shots from the high-danger zone (slot and lower вбрасывание circles) the вратарь stops. On average, about 15–20% of these shots result in goals. A вратарь with HDSV% .820 is in the middle of the pack; .850+ is elite. This number tells far more than the overall result because it specifically evaluates the most critical segment.

4. Low-Danger Save Percentage (LDSV%)

Shots from outside, from the blue line, from tight angles. A league average of .975–.985 is normal; anyone notably below this may have concentration issues in seemingly routine situations. For coaches: analyze whether goals from low-danger zones point to inattention or poor positioning.

5. Rebound Control Rate

How often does a вратарь cleanly secure the puck, rather than letting it rebound uncontrolled? Professional вратарьs control approximately 60–70% of their shots cleanly (no rebound). Rebounds lead directly to the most dangerous follow-up situations. Poor rebound control is often a technical problem: too-shallow pad angle, incorrect hand position, or insufficient "puck absorption" with the glove.

6. Lateral Movement Assessment

The ability to scramble across — quickly changing position on cross-slot passes. Measured in practice through the "post-to-post" drill: time from one post to the other in full save position. A good result for amateurs: under 1.2 seconds with correct technique. Deficiencies here often stem from weak hip flexors and insufficient T-push training.

7. Depth Management (Crease Depth)

Standing too deep in the crease gives the shooter wider angles. Standing too far out means poor reaction time and vulnerability to chips over the shoulder. Optimal depth varies by shot angle. Coaches can measure this easily: cord markings in practice, video analysis from the side angle. Many junior вратарьs stand 15–20 cm too deep, which significantly worsens angle coverage.

8. Screen Situations: SV% in Front of Screens

Shots with a player in front are harder for every вратарь. The difference between elite вратарьs and average ones often lies here: good вратарьs communicate early with their defensemen, actively look for the puck around the screen, and adjust their starting position when screen runs are recognizable. Coaches should explicitly practice screen situations — at least once per week during preparation.

9. Five-Hole Tendency

The five-hole — the area between the pads — is a weak spot for many вратарьs, particularly after quick lateral movements when the pads don't close completely. Shots that draw the вратарь into motion and then finish on the five-hole are nearly impossible for many amateur вратарьs to stop. Analysis: what percentage of goals allowed go through the five-hole? If it's above 25%, targeted pad-closing training is needed.

10. Blocker/Glove Asymmetry

Almost all вратарьs have a weaker side. An asymmetry of more than 8 percentage points between glove-side SV% and blocker-side SV% is a clear training signal. Opponents at higher levels quickly identify such tendencies. Concrete drill: shot series exclusively to the weaker side, with increasing shot speed and variation.

11. Cold Start vs. Hot Goalie

How does performance in the first five minutes of a game compare to the 30-minute mark? Some вратарьs need 8–10 minutes to reach operating temperature — statistically evident in a higher goals-allowed rate in the early minutes. This can be addressed through a specific warm-up routine: more intensive shot series, more lateral movements, explicit mental activation before puck drop.

12. Penalty Shot Performance

Penalty shots result in goals approximately 32% of the time in the NHL — in amateur hockey, this rate varies considerably (20–45%). A вратарь who stops three of five penalty shots has often contributed more to season success than ten extra SV% points in regular play. Training for penalty shots: analyze shooter tendencies (forehand/backhand, speed variations), individual reaction drills, reading hips rather than watching the puck.

  2. Rebound control rate (can be estimated visually, video analysis helpful)
  3. Crease depth (check once per training block with markers)
  4. Blocker/glove asymmetry (assessable after 5–6 games)
  5. Cold-start tendency (compare first 5 minutes vs. rest of game)
