Faceoff gewinnen: Technik, Taktik und Trainingsdrills

In einem typischen Eishockey-Spiel finden 50–70 Faceoffs statt. Teams, die über 55 % ihrer Bullys gewinnen, besitzen messbar mehr Puck-Possession in entscheidenden Phasen. Besonders in der eigenen Defensivzone, im Powerplay und in den letzten zwei Minuten enger Spiele ist der Faceoff-Gewinn direkt mit dem Spielausgang korreliert. Trotzdem wird das Bully-Spiel im Training kaum systematisch geübt. Dieser Artikel ändert das.

Körperhaltung: Die Basis des Faceoff-Spiels

Eine gute Faceoff-Körperhaltung ist der erste Schritt zu mehr Bully-Gewinnen. Die Grundposition: Knie leicht gebeugt (nicht vollständig gestreckt), Körper über den Knien, Gewicht auf den Fußballen, Schulterlinie parallel zur Bully-Linie. Der obere Arm (nahe zum Gegner) ist locker, der untere Arm (Schlägerhand) ist auf Zug bereit.

Fehler, die häufig zu sehen sind: Aufrechte Haltung (zu langsam beim Puck-Drop), zu breiter Stand (schlechte Lateral-Reaktion), Stab zu weit vom Körper (Hebelwirkung fehlt). Die Haltung sollte regelmäßig vor einem Spiegel oder auf Video gecheckt werden.

Griffvarianten: Forehand, Backhand und Schiebegriff

Forehand-Griff

Der obere Hand greift den Schläger wie beim normalen Spielen, Blade auf dem Eis zeigend in Richtung Puck. Vorteil: Natürliche Position, guter Zug nach hinten. Nachteil: Gegner kann leicht lesen, wohin der Puck gespielt wird.

Backhand-Griff

Die obere Hand dreht, sodass die Blade Richtung eigenes Tor zeigt. Vorteil: Überraschend für den Gegner, gut für schnellen Puck hinter die eigene Körperlinie. Nachteil: Weniger Kraft, schwerer zu standardisieren. Am besten beim defensiven Faceoff (eigene Zone).

Schiebegriff (Push-Grip)

Beide Hände halten den Schläger nahe zusammen, Blade wird vorgeschoben statt gezogen. Ziel ist es, den Gegner-Schläger aus der Position zu schieben, bevor er den Puck berührt. Sehr effektiv beim Tie-Up (Schläger verschränken), weil der Gegner seine Blade nicht kontrollieren kann.

Timing: Der entscheidende Moment

Das Faceoff wird nicht nach Reaktion auf den Puck-Drop gewonnen, sondern durch Antizipation. Die besten Faceoff-Spezialisten reagieren nicht auf den fallenden Puck – sie initiieren die Bewegung kurz davor, basierend auf dem Rhythmus des Schiedsrichters. Trainiere: Schiedsrichter-Timing lesen, Handbewegung vor dem Drop beobachten, regelmäßige Dropversuche ohne Gegner zur Schulung des Timings.

Im Spiel: Schau nicht auf den Puck, sondern auf die Hand des Schiedsrichters. Der Puck fällt erst, wenn die Hand aufgeht. Dieser Moment, nicht der Aufprall auf dem Eis, ist das Signal.

Tie-Up-Techniken: Den Gegner neutralisieren

Wenn das Timing nicht perfekt sitzt oder der Gegner im Vorteil ist, ist Tie-Up die sichere Alternative: Schläger des Gegners mit dem eigenen verschränken und dessen Blade kontrollieren. Das verhindert, dass der Gegner den Puck präzise spielt, und gibt den eigenen Teamkollegen mehr Zeit, den Puck aufzunehmen.

Effektive Tie-Up-Sequenz: Blade des Gegners von oben überqueren, nach innen drücken, eigene Blade zwischen Gegner-Blade und Puck schieben. Gleichzeitig: Körperkontakt aufbauen, Gewicht auf den Gegner übertragen, ihm Raum nehmen. Nach dem Tie-Up: Sofort auf die Unterstützung der eigenen Mitspieler (Wingers) verlassen, die den loose Puck aufnehmen.

Augen-Tricks: Psychologische Komponente

Profi-Centerstürmer bestätigen: Der kurze Augenkontakt vor dem Bully signalisiert dem Gegner etwas und verunsichert. Schaue kurz in eine Richtung, spiele den Puck in die andere. Oder: Starrer Blick auf den Puck, dann spontaner Richtungswechsel. Das sind subtile Mittel – aber in 50-50-Situationen macht die Psychologie den Unterschied.

Set Plays nach gewonnenem Faceoff

Offensive Zone: Direkt auf den Abschluss

Nach gewonnenem Bully in der Angriffszone (obere Bullykreise) gibt es drei klare Set-Play-Optionen:

  1. Direkt hinter das Tor: Center zieht den Puck nach hinten, Winger holt ihn auf und dreht. Gegner müssen rotieren – öffnet den Slot.
  2. Zur blauen Linie: Puck sofort nach hinten auf den Point-Spieler, der direkt abschließt oder einen Querpass spielt. Erfordert schnelles Positioning der Verteidiger.
  3. Direkt auf das Tor: Center gewinnt Bully nach vorne, Winger schießt sofort aus dem Bullykreis. Überraschungsmoment ist die Stärke – Torwart nicht positioniert.

Defensive Zone: Sicherheit geht vor

Eigene Zone, Bully tief links oder rechts: Hier ist das erste Ziel, den Puck unter Kontrolle zu bringen oder klar zu spielen – kein riskantes Manöver. Set Plays hier sind einfacher: Puck nach hinten hinter die eigene Torlinie, oder: Sofortiger Clear entlang der Bande. Alle fünf Spieler müssen wissen, wer den Puck nimmt und wer screent.

Neutrale Zone: Übergang schaffen

Bully in der neutralen Zone: Ziel ist Puck-Kontrolle und sofortiger Angriff. Gewinnst du in der Mitte nach vorne, ist ein 2-on-1 oder 3-on-2 möglich, wenn die Flügel ihre Positionen halten. Trainiere: Nach neutralem Faceoff-Sieg sofort Sprints in die Tiefe – der Gegner ist kurz unpositioniert.

5 Trainingsdrills für besseres Faceoff-Spiel

Drill 1: Shadow-Drop (10 Min.)

Zwei Spieler gegenüber, kein Puck. Schiedsrichter simuliert Drop-Bewegung. Spieler reagieren auf Handbewegung, nicht auf Puck. Ziel: Timing schulen.

Drill 2: Tie-Up-Station (10 Min.)

Faceoff-Kreis, Fokus nur auf Tie-Up-Technik. Kein Versuch, den Puck zu gewinnen – nur Gegner-Blade kontrollieren, Körper positionieren. Wer länger kontrolliert, gewinnt.

Drill 3: Set-Play-Faceoff (15 Min.)

5-on-5, jedes Bully hat einen vorher festgelegten Set Play. Center weiß, wohin er spielt. Team weiß, welche Position wer einnimmt. Kein freies Spiel – nur Plan ausführen.

Drill 4: Faceoff-Wettbewerb (10 Min.)

Zwei Center, je 10 Faceoffs. Gewinner spielt im nächsten Übungsspiel; Verlierer nimmt sich eine Aufgabe (z.B. 10 Liegestütze). Wettkampfcharakter erhöht die Konzentration stark.

Drill 5: Positions-Faceoff (15 Min.)

Drei verschiedene Faceoff-Positionen (off-zone, def-zone, neutral) rotierend. Nach jedem gewonnenen Bully muss das Team den Set Play ausführen und das Spiel zu Ende spielen (3 Pässe oder Tor). Trainiert nicht nur das Bully selbst, sondern den Übergang danach.

Winning Faceoffs: Technique, Tactics, and Training Drills

In a typical hockey game, 50–70 faceoffs take place. Teams that win over 55% of their draws measurably control more puck possession during critical phases of play. Especially in the defensive zone, on the power play, and in the final two minutes of close games, winning faceoffs correlates directly with the outcome. Yet systematic faceoff training is rarely part of practice. This article changes that.

Body Position: The Foundation of Faceoff Play

Good faceoff body position is the first step toward winning more draws. The base position: knees slightly bent (not fully extended), body over the knees, weight on the balls of the feet, shoulder line parallel to the dot. The upper arm (facing the opponent) is loose; the lower arm (stick hand) is primed to pull.

Common errors: standing too upright (too slow on the drop), stance too wide (poor lateral reaction), stick too far from the body (loss of leverage). Body position should be checked regularly in front of a mirror or on video.

Grip Variations: Forehand, Backhand, and Push Grip

Forehand Grip

The upper hand grips the stick as in normal play, with the blade pointed toward the puck. Advantage: natural position, good pull-back. Disadvantage: the opponent can easily read where the puck will go.

Backhand Grip

The upper hand rotates so the blade faces toward your own net. Advantage: surprises the opponent, good for a quick puck behind your body line. Disadvantage: less power, harder to standardize. Best used on defensive-zone faceoffs.

Push Grip

Both hands hold the stick close together; the blade is pushed forward rather than pulled. The goal is to sweep the opponent's stick out of position before it contacts the puck. Very effective for tie-ups because the opponent loses blade control.

Timing: The Critical Moment

Faceoffs are not won by reacting to the puck drop — they're won through anticipation. The best faceoff specialists don't react to the falling puck; they initiate movement just before it, based on the referee's rhythm. Train this by reading the referee's timing, watching the hand movement before the drop, and practicing drops without an opponent to develop a feel for timing.

In a game: don't watch the puck — watch the referee's hand. The puck drops when the hand opens. That moment, not the impact on the ice, is your cue.

Tie-Up Techniques: Neutralizing Your Opponent

When your timing isn't perfect or your opponent has the advantage, a tie-up is the safe alternative: cross your stick over the opponent's and control their blade. This prevents the opponent from making a clean play, giving your teammates more time to retrieve the loose puck.

Effective tie-up sequence: cross over the opponent's blade from above, press inward, slide your blade between theirs and the puck. Simultaneously: apply body contact, transfer weight onto the opponent, take away their space. After the tie-up: immediately rely on your wingers to pick up the loose puck.

Eye Tricks: The Psychological Component

Pro centers confirm it: brief eye contact before the draw sends a signal to your opponent and creates doubt. Glance briefly in one direction, play the puck the other way. Or: lock your stare on the puck, then make a sudden change of direction. These are subtle tools — but in 50/50 situations, psychology tips the balance.

Set Plays After a Won Faceoff

Offensive Zone: Going Straight to the Finish

After winning a draw in the offensive zone (upper faceoff circles), there are three clear set play options:

  1. Directly behind the net: Center draws the puck back, winger retrieves and pivots. Defenders must rotate — opens the slot.
  2. To the blue line: Puck immediately back to the point player who shoots directly or moves it across. Requires quick positioning from the defensemen.
  3. Directly on net: Center wins the draw forward, winger shoots immediately from the circle. The element of surprise is the strength — the goalie isn't set.

Defensive Zone: Safety First

In your own zone, deep left or right draws: the primary goal is to control the puck or get a clear — no risky maneuvers. Set plays here are simpler: puck back behind your own goal line, or an immediate clear along the boards. All five players must know who takes the puck and who screens.

Neutral Zone: Creating Transition

Neutral-zone faceoffs: the goal is puck control and an immediate attack. Win it forward and you can create a 2-on-1 or 3-on-2 if your wingers hold their positions. Train this: after a neutral-zone win, immediate sprints into depth — the opponent is briefly out of position.

5 Training Drills for Better Faceoffs

Drill 1: Shadow Drop (10 min.)

Two players face each other, no puck. A coach simulates the drop motion. Players react to the hand movement, not the puck. Goal: develop timing.

Drill 2: Tie-Up Station (10 min.)

In the faceoff circle, focus exclusively on tie-up technique. No attempt to win the puck — only control the opponent's blade and position your body. Whoever maintains control longest wins.

Drill 3: Set-Play Faceoff (15 min.)

5-on-5, each draw has a pre-assigned set play. The center knows exactly where to play it. The team knows each player's assignment. No free play — execute the plan only.

Drill 4: Faceoff Competition (10 min.)

Two centers, 10 draws each. The winner plays in the next scrimmage; the loser does a task (e.g., 10 push-ups). The competitive element dramatically improves focus.

Drill 5: Positional Faceoff (15 min.)

Three different faceoff spots (offensive, defensive, neutral) in rotation. After each won draw, the team executes the set play and plays it out (3 passes or a shot). This trains not only the draw itself, but the transition that follows.

Two players face each other, no puck. A coach simulates the drop motion. Players react to the hand movement, not the puck. Goal: develop timing.

Drill 2: Tie-Up Station (10 min.)

In the aloitus circle, focus exclusively on tie-up technique. No attempt to win the puck — only control the opponent's blade and position your body. Whoever maintains control longest wins.

Drill 3: Set-Play Faceoff (15 min.)

5-on-5, each draw has a pre-assigned set play. The center knows exactly where to play it. The team knows each player's assignment. No free play — execute the plan only.

Drill 4: Faceoff Competition (10 min.)

Two centers, 10 draws each. The winner plays in the next scrimmage; the loser does a task (e.g., 10 push-ups). The competitive element dramatically improves focus.

Drill 5: Positional Faceoff (15 min.)

Three different aloitus spots (offensive, defensive, neutral) in rotation. After each won draw, the team executes the set play and plays it out (3 passes or a shot). This trains not only the draw itself, but the transition that follows.

Как выигрывать вбрасывания: техника, тактика и тренировочные упражнения

In a typical hockey game, 50–70 вбрасывания take place. Teams that win over 55% of their draws measurably control more puck possession during critical phases of play. Especially in the defensive zone, on the большинство, and in the final two minutes of close games, winning вбрасывания correlates directly with the outcome. Yet systematic вбрасывание training is rarely part of practice. This article changes that.

Body Position: The Foundation of Faceoff Play

Good вбрасывание body position is the first step toward winning more draws. The base position: knees slightly bent (not fully extended), body over the knees, weight on the balls of the feet, shoulder line parallel to the dot. The upper arm (facing the opponent) is loose; the lower arm (stick hand) is primed to pull.

Common errors: standing too upright (too slow on the drop), stance too wide (poor lateral reaction), stick too far from the body (loss of leverage). Body position should be checked regularly in front of a mirror or on video.

Grip Variations: Forehand, Backhand, and Push Grip

Forehand Grip

The upper hand grips the stick as in normal play, with the blade pointed toward the puck. Advantage: natural position, good pull-back. Disadvantage: the opponent can easily read where the puck will go.

Backhand Grip

The upper hand rotates so the blade faces toward your own net. Advantage: surprises the opponent, good for a quick puck behind your body line. Disadvantage: less power, harder to standardize. Best used on defensive-zone вбрасывания.

Push Grip

Both hands hold the stick close together; the blade is pushed forward rather than pulled. The goal is to sweep the opponent's stick out of position before it contacts the puck. Very effective for tie-ups because the opponent loses blade control.

Timing: The Critical Moment

Вбрасывания are not won by reacting to the puck drop — they're won through anticipation. The best вбрасывание specialists don't react to the falling puck; they initiate movement just before it, based on the referee's rhythm. Train this by reading the referee's timing, watching the hand movement before the drop, and practicing drops without an opponent to develop a feel for timing.

In a game: don't watch the puck — watch the referee's hand. The puck drops when the hand opens. That moment, not the impact on the ice, is your cue.

Tie-Up Techniques: Neutralizing Your Opponent

When your timing isn't perfect or your opponent has the advantage, a tie-up is the safe alternative: cross your stick over the opponent's and control their blade. This prevents the opponent from making a clean play, giving your teammates more time to retrieve the loose puck.

Effective tie-up sequence: cross over the opponent's blade from above, press inward, slide your blade between theirs and the puck. Simultaneously: apply body contact, transfer weight onto the opponent, take away their space. After the tie-up: immediately rely on your wingers to pick up the loose puck.

Eye Tricks: The Psychological Component

Pro centers confirm it: brief eye contact before the draw sends a signal to your opponent and creates doubt. Glance briefly in one direction, play the puck the other way. Or: lock your stare on the puck, then make a sudden change of direction. These are subtle tools — but in 50/50 situations, psychology tips the balance.

Set Plays After a Won Faceoff

Offensive Zone: Going Straight to the Finish

After winning a draw in the offensive zone (upper вбрасывание circles), there are three clear set play options:

  1. Directly behind the net: Center draws the puck back, winger retrieves and pivots. Defenders must rotate — opens the slot.
  2. To the blue line: Puck immediately back to the point player who shoots directly or moves it across. Requires quick positioning from the defensemen.
  3. Directly on net: Center wins the draw forward, winger shoots immediately from the circle. The element of surprise is the strength — the вратарь isn't set.

Defensive Zone: Safety First

In your own zone, deep left or right draws: the primary goal is to control the puck or get a clear — no risky maneuvers. Set plays here are simpler: puck back behind your own goal line, or an immediate clear along the boards. All five players must know who takes the puck and who screens.

Neutral Zone: Creating Transition

Neutral-zone вбрасывания: the goal is puck control and an immediate attack. Win it forward and you can create a 2-on-1 or 3-on-2 if your wingers hold their positions. Train this: after a neutral-zone win, immediate sprints into depth — the opponent is briefly out of position.
